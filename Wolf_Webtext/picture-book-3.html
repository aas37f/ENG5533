Flossie and the Fox

Patricia C. McKissak’s Flossie and the Fox (1986), although intended for a slightly older audience of 5-8 years of age, functions well as an early education read-aloud as its dialogue is rather complex. The main characters, Flossie, who speaks AAE, and the fox, who speaks very proper MAE, converse about why Flossie cannot recognize the fox as a fox (which ends up being a trick to get him to follow her to his demise by the blood hounds). The narration features classic AAE elements, such as in the clipping of the gerund in “Big Mama stopped sortin’ her peaches” and the occasional omission of the past tense, such as in “she say, reaching behind her.” Flossie’s speech typically omits any conjugation of the verb “to be” other than the word be, seen in “I be Flossie” and “I reckon I don’t know who you be.” She also changes her second-person pronouns from you to ya or yo, such as in “You sho’ think a heap of yo’self” and “Why should I be scared o’ ya?”. She also removes voiced and unvoiced dentals from the ends of her words, saying “you aine no fox” (ain’t) and “I am just a chile” (child). AAE is reflected both in Flossie’s speech and the narration of the story, which makes the Fox’s MAE dialect distinctly out of place. Only Flossie and her mother speak AAE, elevating the dialect above MAE; the failure of the fox sets the girl’s knowledge above the fox’s— and in turn, her dialect above MAE.
